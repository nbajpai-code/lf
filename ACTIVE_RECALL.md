# 🎯 Active Recall — The #1 Learning Technique

> *"The act of retrieving a memory changes the memory itself — making it stronger."* — Henry Roediger

---

## What It Is

**Active Recall** (also called Retrieval Practice) means pulling information from memory **without looking at your notes**. Instead of re-reading or re-watching, you force your brain to reconstruct what you've learned.

It is considered the **single most evidence-supported learning technique** in cognitive psychology.

---

## Why It Works — The Testing Effect

Decades of research (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006) demonstrate that:
- **Re-reading**: Students believe it works but it produces minimal retention
- **Testing/Recall**: Produces 2–3× better long-term retention than re-reading

The struggle to retrieve a memory **strengthens the neural pathways** connecting that knowledge, making future retrieval faster and more reliable.

> Even getting the answer **wrong** during practice improves learning — the error creates a prediction signal that drives memory consolidation.

## 🛠️ Active Recall Methods

### 1. The Blank Page Method
After reading/watching a section:
1. Close all materials
2. On a blank page, write everything you can recall about the topic
3. Review your source and identify gaps
4. Focus study on the gaps

### 2. Question-First Reading
Before reading a chapter:
- Turn all headings into questions
- Read to answer those questions
- After reading, answer the questions without looking

### 3. Flashcards (Physical or Digital)
The classic retrieval tool. See [Spaced Repetition](SPACED_REPETITION.md) for the optimal scheduling.

### 4. The 50% Rule
Spend only 50% of learning time consuming content. Spend the other 50% actively recalling it.

### 5. Practice Testing
Take practice exams, coding challenges, or mock interviews before you feel ready. The difficulty of early testing accelerates learning.

### 6. The "Brain Dump" Daily Habit
At the end of each day: write everything you learned (without notes). 5 minutes, zero structure. Pure recall.

### 7. Teach or Explain (→ Feynman)
Explaining to someone else is forced active recall + gap identification. See [Feynman Technique](FEYNMAN_TECHNIQUE.md).

## 📊 Retention Study Comparison

| Method | 1-Week Retention |
|---|---|
| Re-reading | ~28% |
| Concept mapping | ~45% |
| Interleaved practice | ~54% |
| **Active recall (testing)** | **~65–80%** |
| **Active recall + Spaced repetition** | **~80–90%** |

*Source: Adapted from Roediger, Karpicke et al.*

## 🤖 AI Prompts for Active Recall

```
I just finished studying [TOPIC]. Quiz me on it.
Ask me 8 questions of increasing difficulty.
After I answer each one, tell me what I got right and what's missing.
Don't give me the answer before I try.
```

```
I'm about to study [TOPIC]. Before I read anything, 
ask me 5 questions to activate my prior knowledge and 
surface what I think I already know. Note any likely misconceptions.
```
